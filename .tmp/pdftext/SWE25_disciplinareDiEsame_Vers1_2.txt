some general (but crucial) rules

 this is a self-assigned work to focus exam discussion
• it is intended to have a concrete basis on which we can discuss,
a kind of "free question" (domanda a scelta)
• for the discussion, the candidates must have
full understanding and mastery on everything appears in the work
• the work does not replace general understanding of course contents: the
candidate should first go through and understand course contents,
and after that the self-assigned work becomes a concrete occasion to bring
them in action in the design and production of something
 review
• you may want to ask a feedback on the assumed statement,
and artifacts produced along the development
• I’ll try to answer, with best effort, but this should not block your progress
• feedback shall be asked on written concepts, trying to focus specific points
of attention … this is essential to permit concrete answers

some general (but crucial) rules

 Generative AI
• the use of Generative AI is welcome and promoted, not requested
• proficient usage of generative AI is a value, though not requested
• document any usage you made: for which parts of the work, with which
process of integration with your integrated development environment, ...
• quality and detail of this documentation is part of the value of the work
• be able to understand and discuss choices made by the generator
 do NOT generate UML from the code, this is normally of no use
 do NOT generate text in the relation, generated text is not acceptable when it
is expected to be read by humans (myself, in this case)

three kinds of project work

 There are 3 different types of project work,
accommodating different starting levels and aims of the candidate
• Mode #2 is the common case
• Mode #1 simplifies the objective
• Mode#3 makes the objective more ambitious
 Mode#2 is the common and most frequent case.
• It consists in design and production of a fragment of application,
referred to functions in some application domain outlined in a statement,
developed so as to give space to practices of SW illustrated in the course;
• It can be conveniently developed by a team of 2,3 students, sometimes 4,
or also by a single student.
• The way how to identify the subject and good practices for its development
are specified in the sequel of this document

three kinds of project work

 Mode#1 simplifies the objective
• it is an exercise of OO design, development and testing,
typically centered on the combination of 2-3 design patterns
motivated in some imaginary, not necessairly realistic application scenario
• (some examples are partially developed in the slides on Design Patterns,
e.g. combining Composite, Observer, Adapter, Decorator, Strategy …)
• Mode#1 does not bring in action all the contents of the course,
but it still permits to consolidate crucial abilities to develope and test code
using the Java language and its effective idioms,
combining OO design principles and UML models
• this is less ambitious than Mode#2 and attains a weaker proficiency,
but it is the recommanded choice when starting from a weak level of
programming skill
• It is normally (but not always) developed by a single candidate

three kinds of project work

 Mode#3 is a kind of special project work
• it is intended for candidates with a good level in software development,
who may be interested in applying this skill
in the solution of some advanced computing problem
• possible subjects include the development of quantitative analytics
integrating probabilistic models, or in the experimentation of advanced
practices of software engineering, or in the development of code that
integrates LLM-powered agents, …
• it is normally (but not always) developed by a team of 2,3 students,
and its contents are defined case by case depending on the specific
interests and skills of candidates

Organization of the report

 The report normally includes these sections
• statement, architecture at glance, used technologies and tools
• requirements analysis and functional design, use case diags, templates,
mockups, page navigation diagram
• design, architecture, package diagram, class diags for each package, details
of specific significance
• implementation details, responsibilities, snippets and discussion on
elements of specific relevance, idioms, design patterns, architectural
patterns, details on used technologies
• tests, functional (black box, integration) and structural (white box, unit)
• (see more in examples in the sequel)

Requirements analysis



Use Case Diagrams + Templates + Mockups + Navigation Diagram
• Use Case Diagrams and templates are mandatory
• mockups and page navigation diagram are recommended
• associate mockups with pages in the page navigation diag
(whenever these are developed)



In Use Case Diagram
• consider to annotate with a stereotype Use Cases that have level <<function>> or
<<summary>>, so that all the remaining ones are considered to be <<user goal>>
• better use annotations <<include>> and <<extend>> annotations,
and use <<precedes>> and <<invokes>> only in special cases



In templates, some fields have higher relevance
• number, title, level, roles, normal and alternative flows, pre- and post-conditions
• be able to distinguish whether the level is "user goal", "function", or "summary"
• maintain consistency between titles and numbers in use case diags and templates (to this
end, consider add numbers in the use case diagram)
• steps in the normal flow and alternative flows can be associated with (available) mockups
• normal flow and alternative flows can be associated with tests
